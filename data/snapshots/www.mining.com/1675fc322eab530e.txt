Lithium price sinks to five-month low as restarts stoke 2027 glut fears
Lithium futures in China sank to a five-month low on Tuesday as expectations that a wave of restarted mines will tip the market back into oversupply next year overshadowed strong near-term demand from the electric vehicle and energy storage sectors.
The most active lithium carbonate contract on the Guangzhou Futures Exchange fell to 136,800 yuan ($20,210) a tonne, its lowest since February 10, before clawing back part of the loss in afternoon trade to close at 144,000 yuan, still down 4.95% on the day. The contract edged down a further 0.7% to 143,000 yuan ($21,126) on Wednesday.
The contract has now given back nearly 30% from the two-year high above 200,000 yuan reached in mid-May, although it still trades at more than double its level of a year ago, before the shutdown of CATL's giant Jianxiawo mine set off a months-long rally. Even after Tuesday's drop, lithium remains up about 22% in 2026.
Tuesday's slide also lands just as Beijing works to make Guangzhou the reference point for global lithium pricing. The exchange opened its lithium carbonate contract to overseas traders this month and plans to launch a second futures contract, for lithium hydroxide, this year in a push for more pricing power over the battery metal.
US stockpiling
Washington has also handed lithium bulls a fresh argument earlier this month with a solicitation posted by the US Defense Logistics Agency asking suppliers for fixed prices on almost 36 million pounds, about 16,000 tonnes, of battery-grade lithium carbonate for delivery over five years, a purchase worth as much as $300 million for the national defense stockpile.
Bids closed on Friday, with the tender requiring processing and testing of the material to happen entirely within the continental United States.
The purchase, which at the full tonnage works out to roughly $18,500 a tonne, well below even Wednesday's depressed price in Guangzhou, is part of a wider US push on critical minerals that includes Project Vault, a $12 billion stockpiling initiative for the private sector and a broader rebuild of Pentagon metal reserves that were sold down over decades.
For the lithium market the volumes are modest, spread over five years the purchase equals only a few days of global consumption, but the tender puts Washington in the market as a buyer for the first time in the battery era.
Supply restarts
That rally is now unwinding as the supply that idled during the downturn comes back. CATL restarted Jianxiawo in late June after securing a new safety permit, returning about 46,000 tonnes of annual capacity, some 3% of global output, to a market that had priced in its absence.
Australian producers are following. Mineral Resources is restarting its Bald Hill operation, Core Lithium is bringing its Finniss project back online, and the Mt Marion joint venture has approved a A$490 million expansion, moves that analysts expect to swell supply through 2027 after two years in which low prices had forced curtailments across the industry.
Not every 2027 signal points the same way. Zimbabwe, which supplied about 10% of the world's mined lithium last year, on Friday rejected industry calls to delay the ban on lithium concentrate exports it will impose from January 1.
"We are still sticking with January 1," mines minister Polite Kambamura told Bloomberg reporters. "They have to run with this."
Demand still running hot
The retreat comes despite little sign of weakness on the demand side. China's output of power and energy storage batteries reached 191.7 GWh in May, up more than 55% from a year earlier, and the boom in grid-scale energy storage has continued to absorb material alongside resilient electric vehicle sales.
Beijing is set to tax the boom. China will levy a 2% consumption tax on battery products, including primary lithium batteries and lithium-ion batteries, from September 1, with the rate doubling to 4% a year later. Goldman Sachs said anticipation of the tax could pull demand forward ahead of the implementation date.
When Ganfeng Lithium chairman Li Liangbin predicted in November that booming demand could carry lithium carbonate to 200,000 yuan a tonne, the forecast looked bold. The market briefly delivered exactly that in May, and has been retreating since.
Lithium equities have taken a harder beating than the metal as the rally unwound. Over the past month Liontown Resources lost 36% in Sydney, PLS, the former Pilbara Minerals, fell 26%, Mineral Resources 21%, Core Lithium 20% and IGO 18%, while Ganfeng Lithium dropped 36% in Hong Kong.
Among US-listed names, Lithium Americas gave up 28%, Albemarle 22%, Sigma Lithium 21% and SQM 11%, with the Global X Lithium ETF down 15%. Sydney's producers fell again on Wednesday, Liontown by a further 5.1%, although the US names steadied in early trade.
