Yemen's Iran-backed Houthi rebels have bombed two Saudi oil tankers off the coast of Saudi Arabia, threatening to further disrupt oil supply in a sign that the war is spreading across the region.
The Houthis claimed an attack on two vessels 70 nautical miles southwest of the port of Al Shuqaiq, saying they had violated a maritime blockade.
The incident had earlier been reported by the United Kingdom Maritime Trade Operations (UKMTO), which said crews were attempting to extinguish a fire onboard. No casualties or environmental impact have been reported.
According to the Saudi Press Agency (SPA), which cited an official from the General Transport Authority, the incident caused a fire at the front of the ship.
It came after Iran's foreign minister warned of an "eye for an eye" response if Donald Trump continues with his threat to bomb a bridge or power plant for every ship targeted in the Strait of Hormuz.
The US military said it had carried out a 12th straight night of strikes against Iran on Thursday.
Major security gathering in Southeast Asia focusses on Middle East
Top diplomats from Asia, Europe and North America met at the security-focused ASEAN Regional Forum, where ministers were expected to call for a peaceful settlement of renewed and intensifying hostilities in the Middle East.
The war in Iran and the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz are set to dominate the Manila meetings attended by US secretary of state Marco Rubio, Russian foreign minister Sergei Lavrov and top officials from Australia, Britain, China, the European Union, India, Japan and others.
The conflict is a key concern for the 11-nation Association of Southeast Asian Nations, which has a combined gross domestic product of about $3.8 trillion (£2.8 trillion) heavily reliant on Middle East oil, exposing it to fallout that has stoked global inflation.
"We convene against the backdrop of profound geopolitical and geoeconomic uncertainty," Maria Theresa Lazaro, the foreign secretary of the Philippines, which is chairing the grouping, said in opening remarks.
Iran's Revolutionary Guard claims it carried out attacks on US assets in Jordan
Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) claimed it carried out attacks on US military assets in Jordan, saying it "destroyed a radar of the American THAAD missile defence system with lightning strikes".
It said a "Patriot system and a C-RAM radar were also targeted and destroyed, and the fuel tanks of the American base were set on fire".
It added that "a large helicopter equipment warehouse and a helicopter repair and maintenance shed were also set on fire and destroyed".
The IRGC said the strikes were aimed at US forces, not Jordan, and argued they were a response to American attacks on Iran.
It claimed the operations did not violate Jordan's sovereignty.
Jordan had not immediately commented on the claims.
The IRGC had earlier also claimed attacks on US military targets in Kuwait.
US uses B-1 long-range bomber for first time since fighting against Iran resumed
The US has escalated its military campaign against Iran by using a B-1 long-range bomber for the first time since fighting resumed 12 days ago, according to US officials.
The bomber was used to strike IRGC targets in Iran, Axios reported. B-1 aircraft can carry large numbers of heavy bombs and cruise missiles.
US Centcom said its strikes targeted Iranian military operations centres, maritime capabilities, aircraft hangars, drone storage facilities and logistics infrastructure to weaken Tehran's ability to threaten commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz.
However, it remains unclear what specific targets the B-1 hit.
Regional mediators push for new 10-day ceasefire between US and Iran
Qatar, Pakistan and Egypt have proposed a 10-day ceasefire between the US and Iran in an effort to prevent the conflict from spreading into a wider regional war, Axios reported.
The proposal comes after continuous US strikes on Iran and continued Iranian attacks targeting Gulf states, including Kuwait, Bahrain and Jordan.
Iran has vowed an "eye for an eye" response after US president Donald Trump threatened strikes on Iranian infrastructure for attacks on ships in the Strait of Hormuz.

Iranian state media report two dead in latest US strikes
Iranian state media reported that two people were killed in US strikes in southern Iran, near the country's border with Iraq.
The deaths were reported as part of the latest wave of American attacks, which the US military said targeted "maritime capabilities, missile and drone storage facilities, coastal surveillance sites, and air defence assets".
Centcom said in a statement on X: "The strikes further degrade Iran's ability to attack civilian mariners and commercial vessels."
According to Al Jazeera, explosions were reported in cities including Bushehr, Ahvaz, Sirik and Shalamcheh.
Centcom earlier today posted on X, saying it completed the 12th night of strikes on Iran.
Araghchi describes moment of US-Israeli strike that killed Ali Khamenei
Iran's foreign minister Abbas Araghchi has described surviving a US-Israeli strike that killed Ali Khamenei on the first day of the war, saying he was pulled from rubble after a leadership compound was hit.
In an interview with Iranian journalist Javad Mogouyi, Araghchi said the US and Israel had accurate intelligence on the location of Iran's top officials.
"What matters, in my view, is the fact that Israel had intelligence about these meetings," Araghchi said, adding: "This may point to a security gap that probably still exists. This needs to be fixed."
He said he has since been constantly moving around Tehran for security reasons.
Describing the strike, he said: "We were on the other side of the building, and after the explosion, the ceiling, walls, and everything collapsed on us. I grabbed Mr Hijazi's hand (Khamenei's deputy chief of staff, Ali Asghar Hijazi), and we made our way through the beams and boards and pieces of wood, hearing the cries for help."
He continued: "Water was pouring from the ceiling. Everything was wrecked. We eventually got out, and I saw that various parts of the compound had been targeted."
House passes defense bill with Trump's $1T spending request and Department of War rename
The US House of Representatives has approved a defense policy bill that includes President Donald Trump's request for a substantial $1.15 trillion in national security spending and proposes renaming the Department of Defense to the Department of War.
The legislation, which also mandates a 5 percent to 7 percent pay increase for service members next year based on rank, marks a significant departure from the typically bipartisan nature of the annual National Defense Authorization Act.
The vote, a narrow 216-212, underscored deep divisions, with Democrats voicing strong opposition. Their concerns centered on the considerable spending hike for the Pentagon, particularly as Republicans simultaneously sought to cut numerous non-defense programs through other legislative efforts.
