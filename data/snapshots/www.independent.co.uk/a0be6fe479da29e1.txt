The United States is said to be preparing for greater escalation with Iran following days of strikes as both sides wrestle for control of the Strait of Hormuz.
An American official familiar with internal discussions told the Washington Post that the US is still "planning for a wider war" by rushing military aircraft to the region as hopes of a return to diplomacy fade.
The official, speaking on condition of anonymity, warned that US operations would be limited by low stockpiles of air defences and long-range munitions, however.
The US will also struggle to send troops and aircraft to the region due to battle damage, the official said.
US forces hit Iran for a ninth consecutive day on Monday as Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) said they had targeted American assets in Jordan, Kuwait and Syria.
Sirens also sounded in Bahrain on Monday morning, as the Kuwaiti army said it was intercepting hostile drones in an Iranian attack.
Iran still hearing from mediators: foreign ministry
Iran continues to receive messages from intermediaries despite the apparent halt to talks aimed at ending the war, the foreign ministry said today.
Spokesperson Esmail Baghaei rejected the dichotomy of war or diplomacy and said Iran would use both as a "tool" to pursue its national interests.
Iran to 'pursue diplomacy based on its national interests'
Tehran pursues war and diplomacy based on its national interests, Iran's foreign ministry spokesperson said on Monday, after being asked whether the door to talks with the U.S. was now closed.
"I reject the duality of either war or diplomacy. Diplomacy is a tool through which we pursue our national interests, just like war," Esmaeil Baghaei said.
Recap: Two US service members killed by Iranian attacks in Jordan, military says
At least two U.S. service members were killed in action while defending against Iranian ballistic missile and drone attacks in Jordan, marking the first American deaths in the aftermath of a tenuous ceasefire agreement earlier this year, according to U.S. Central Command.
Another service member is missing after Friday's attacks, according to the Department of Defense.
Oil prices up 2 per cent as Iran and US inch closer to all out war
Oil prices jumped more than 2% on Monday, with Brent crude above $90 a barrel as the U.S. and Iran moved closer to resuming an all-out war.
Brent crude, the international standard, rose 2.6% to $90.40 per barrel and benchmark U.S. crude climbed 2.2% to $83.58 per barrel.
Early Monday, the U.S. announced more attacks for a ninth straight night. Iran has been responding to strikes by hitting U.S.-allies across the Middle East.
"The U.S. and Iran continue to exchange strikes, which are proving to be deadly for both sides," ING commodities strategists Warren Patterson and Ewa Manthey wrote in a commentary Monday.
"If this escalation goes unchecked, we could return to an environment of wide-scale attacks across the Persian Gulf."

Gulf states intercept drones and missiles as attacks intensify
Bahrain, Kuwait and Jordan reported intercepting Iranian drones and missiles as attacks across the region intensified.
Bahrain's ministry of interior activated warning sirens and urged residents to "remain calm and head to the nearest safe place", while the US embassy in Manama warned American citizens that Iran may be planning attacks on the capital and advised them to take precautionary measures.
Kuwait's military said it was responding to a series of drone attacks, while Jordan reported intercepting at least three missiles, with another landing in an open area.
The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) claimed it had targeted a weapons depot and air defence radar system in Kuwait, though Kuwaiti authorities have not confirmed those claims and instead said they were dealing with the aftermath of an attack on a power and water desalination facility.
The strike on civilian infrastructure has drawn condemnation from countries across the region.
Oil prices climb above $90 a barrel as US-Iran tensions escalate
Oil prices climbed above $90 a barrel as escalating US-Iran tensions disrupted concerns over crude supplies through the Strait of Hormuz, a key global energy route.
Brent crude rose more than 2 per cent to $90.19 a barrel, reaching its highest level since 11 June, while US West Texas Intermediate (WTI) also gained to $84.20 a barrel.
The latest surge followed the US carrying out a ninth consecutive night of strikes on Iran, along with reports of fresh Iranian attacks on Gulf states.
Shipping data shows traffic through Strait of Hormuz remains limited
Shipping data showed that traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remained limited over the weekend as the US and Iran intensified their attacks, according to Sky News.
According to LSEG data, only four vessels crossed the strategic waterway on Sunday, down from eight the previous day.
Since Friday, at least three oil tankers and one Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) have entered the strait to load oil.
The LSEG data tracks the movement of various types of vessels, including container ships, bulk carriers, vehicle carriers, and tankers transporting oil, gas and chemicals.
