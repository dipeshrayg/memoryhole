The U.S. military began conducting another wave of strikes against Iran on Sunday evening, U.S. Central Command has announced, marking the ninth consecutive night of attacks by American forces.
"The strikes will continue degrading Iranian military capabilities used to attack commercial vessels and civilian mariners transiting the Strait of Hormuz," officials said Sunday.
Returning from the World Cup final, President Donald Trump said the attacks on Iran were in honor of "great patriots" in the America military that were killed in action in recent days.
"We hit them very hard again tonight," Trump told reporters.
The missions come a day after a US service member was killed during the detonation of unexploded Iranian ordnance in northern Iraq.
On Friday, two US service members were killed and another went missing during an Iranian attack in Jordan. Military personnel have recovered remains that Trump suspects are those of a third service member killed in the strike.
Meanwhile, Tehran claimed on Sunday that two US-backed tankers have been involved in an "accident" while attempting to traverse the Strait of Hormuz.
Iran's Revolutionary Guards said two ships had ignored warnings and tried to navigate the Strait via an "unsafe route" while two other vessels abandoned that route, the semi-official Tasnim news agency reported.
UK defence secretary pays tribute to US service personnel killed in Jordan
UK defence secretary Dan Jarvis has commented on the deaths of two US service personnel killed in action in Jordan, saying: "My thoughts are with the families and friends of the US service personnel killed and injured in Jordan."
Four service members were evacuated to Jordanian hospitals for treatment and one service member is missing.
US 'planning for a wider war' but lacks munitions: report
Sunday's round of US strikes on Iran likely won't be the last, amid renewed hostilities and a string of American military deaths.
"The US is planning for a wider war," an unnamed US official familiar with the Trump administration's strategy told The Washington Post.
The official added that these plans come despite dwindling stores of air defense and long-range munitions, as well as degraded capacities to surge troops and aircraft.
"We do not have enough to safely sustain operations, and I don't think the White House is aware of that," the official said.
Trump defends delays in ending Iran conflict: 'This is a far bigger job'
President Donald Trump on Sunday fired back at a journalist who asked about his prior claim the Iran conflict would be finished in a matter of weeks.
"This is a far bigger job that we're doing," the Republican said from the tarmac as he returned from the World Cup final.
"It's really not the same thing," he added of the evolving mission in the Iran conflict, which began in February. "What we're doing now is we're ending any chance where they could have a nuclear missile."
The US previously claimed its June 2025 strikes on Iran "obliterated" the country's nuclear program.
On Sunday, the president painted a modestly different picture of Tehran's military capacities.
"They've lost everything, almost, military," he said. "They've got very little left. They've got some missiles. They've got some drones. They've got some manufacturing ability, not much. But we control the Strait. They don't control anything."
UAE strongly condemns the renewed Iran attacks
The United Arab Emirates issued a further statement on Sunday condemning the escalating strikes by Iran on Gulf states and Jordan.
In a post on X, the ministry of foreign affairs said that the "hostile attacks" on Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan "constitute a flagrant violation of the sovereignty of the brotherly nations and a threat to their security and stability".
Trump claims Iran strikes in honor of slain US service members
President Donald Trump provided a brief update on Sunday about the latest round of strikes against Iran as he headed back to the White House after attending the World Cup final.
"We hit them very hard again tonight, and we did that in honor of the probably three - it's probably three, as opposed to two - great patriots," Trump said from the tarmac.
U.S. Central Command has announced three service member deaths in recent days, including two military members dead after an Iranian attack on Friday in Jordan and a third fatality in Iraq as personnel sought to disable unexploded Iranian ordnance.
A fourth service member is missing as a result of the attack in Jordan.
"We feel very badly, but you know those great people, great patriots, were out there fighting [so] that Iran cannot have a nuclear weapon," Trump said of the deaths.
The resulting retaliatory strikes have left Iran "very, very badly damaged," Trump added, though he often insists Iran has been all but decimated.
Explosions heard in five Iranian cities as US signals latest barrage: reports
Explosions were heard across at least five Iranian cities today, according to the semi-official Iranian news agency Tasnim, as the US announced another night of attacks.
Witnesses and local reporters told Tasnim of explosions in Tabriz, Chabahar, Konarak, Bandar Mahshahr and Bandar Imam Khomeini.
US Central Command said earlier Sunday that renewed strikes began around 7 p.m. Eastern time.

In depth: Nigerian children pay the price for the Iran war as malnutrition and poverty surge
Maryam Aminu was hardly surprised when the last of her six children was diagnosed with malnutrition in April for a second time. She was barely feeding the 18-month-old regularly, let alone with nutritious foods, writes Ope Adetayo.
Although the family in northwest Nigeria has struggled with economic hardship, their situation deteriorated after February, when her husband, Shehu Aminu, lost his job as a taxi driver due to a spike in the retail price of petrol caused by the war in Iran.
"When she was diagnosed the second time, even though I suspected it, I was sad and angry because I knew why," Aminu said in the living area of their unvarnished two-bedroom house in the quiet town of Kware, Sokoto, as ash from the coal stove billowed into the room. "Times are tough, and the food is not consistent."
Children relapsing into malnutrition has become an increasingly common occurrence in the state and across northern Nigeria, according to local health and aid workers, who cite knock-on effects from the Iran war.
Northern Nigeria, one of the world's poorest regions, is already under pressure from an insurgency crisis. Now, the conflict in the Middle East has worsened food security for millions of people living in poverty, especially children.
