Analysis: Be prepared to be shocked and amazed by Burnham's cabinet picks
The one truism of reshuffles and new governments being formed is that the briefings which take place before over who gets what job are often wrong.
A lot of it is based on individuals wanting to get their names out there or supporters of a certain faction wanting their person in a specific role.
What it means is that we really know nothing until the appointment has been made. So it is quite possible that Shabana Mahmood as chancellor and Ed Miliband as foreign secretary could end up being hot air.
The fact that at least six names have been attached to the home secretary job is indicative of the guessing game going on.
But what is more interesting are the surprise names which suddenly pop up and are on nobody's radar.
Infamously in David Cameron's first reshuffle in 2012, Maria Miller walked up Downing Street and none of the journalists outside recognised her with some thinking she was a civil servant. She popped out an hour later as culture secretary.
The most predictable cabinet was Keir Starmer's in 2024 when he appointed his entire shadow cabinet to their respective roles in government. Mr Burnham's is on the opposite end of predictability so prepare to be shocked and amazed.

Analysis: Burnham's home secretary choice will be crucial - but remains a mystery
There have been a large number of briefings over who Andy Burnham will pick as his chancellor - probably home secretary Shabana Mahmood - and foreign secretary - one of the two Milibands, probably Ed - but very little on the third major office of state, home secretary.
With Ms Mahmood expected to leave the role, Mr Burnham has a huge choice to make which will tell us everything about the direction of his government. Given how important immigration, policing and culture wars are for Nigel Farage and Reform UK the decision will be crucial.
He could pick a home secretary from the party's soft left to indicate a more relaxed approach to immigration after telling the party on Friday that "we cannot out Reform Reform."
A number of names have been mentioned in this regard including former deputy PM Angela Rayner, and culture secretary Lisa Nandy.
He could continue the hardline crackdown approach of Ms Mahmood with a choice like former health secretary Wes Streeting or welfare secretary Pat McFadden.
Or he could go for a solid, steady as it goes compromise pick like former defence secretary John Healey or bringing back foreign secretary Yvette Cooper.
All these names have been linked to the role but none seem to have stuck. Yet whoever lands the job will tell us a lot about how Mr Burnham plans to deal with immigration, policing and the whole so-called culture wars agenda.

Andy Burnham to enter No 10 promising 'breathing room' on cost of living
Andy Burnham will enter 10 Downing Street promising to give people "breathing room" against the rising cost of living.
In his first speech as prime minister, Mr Burnham will urge the country to be honest about the challenges it faces and call for "reflection and resolution".
He will also say he is "acutely conscious" that the UK has had seven prime ministers in the last decade, stressing the need for more stable and responsible politics and pledging to restore faith in government.
Allies of Mr Burnham said he will argue on Monday that political stability must deliver "tangible" improvements along with the "breathing room" on the cost of living he has previously promised.
Before the speech, Sir Keir Starmer is expected to make his own address outside No 10 before heading to Buckingham Palace to formally resign before Mr Burnham meets the King to "kiss hands" and be appointed prime minister.
Unlike Sir Keir's statement announcing his resignation, neither speech is likely to be interrupted by protesters after the police banned amplified music outside Downing Street during the handover.
After his speech, Mr Burnham is expected to name his cabinet.
Left-wing Labour MP 'happy' with whoever is chancellor - despite refusing to back Mahmood last week
Dawn Butler has insisted she is "happy" with whoever Andy Burnham decides to appoint as his chancellor, despite refusing to back Shabanna Mahmood last week.
The Labour MP, who sits on the left of the party, last week refused to back Mahmood for chancellor multiple times when asked for her opinion on the potential appointment.
But on Monday, she told Sky News: "I'm happy. No matter who Andy puts in whatever position, I will work with whoever's in that position."
She added: "We've got a Labour prime minister and Andy Burnham is going to be prime minister. I'm very happy."
Asked if it was inevitable that one side of the party would be upset with Mr Burnham's decision, she said: "I'm not quite sure one half the party will be upset because if you look at the transition from here to Andy, it has been a smooth transition."
